          the alterations that occur in the concentrations of protein and       bicarbonate ion when whole blood is cooled are reviewed, together with  their influence on 'correction factors' . the reasons why these factors should not be applied to blood when the living body is undergoing       hypothermia involving the use of an extra-corporeal circulation are     discussed .                                                               examples of the numerical values for blood ph during the two main     methods for producing profound hypothermia, one using autogenous lung   perfusion and the other a pump-oxygenator are given, with reference to  the influence of pco2 upon these values .                                 the existing methods of acid-base measurement appear to be            sufficiently accurate to reflect the metabolic component during profoundhypothermia .
